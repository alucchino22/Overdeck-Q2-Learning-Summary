Innovation

AI is most valuable in education when it augments human judgment — not when it replaces it or remains a standalone tool

Signal strength
Very strong

The strongest AI signals across grantees share a common structure: AI works when it shortens feedback loops, surfaces actionable signals, and frees up human capacity for higher-order work. EdReports found automation was most useful when paired with expert workflow redesign. Saga's HAT model improved only when coaches used AI signals to make decisions. Teaching Lab's Nisa supports coaches rather than replacing them. Stanford's AI writing study is explicitly testing where higher automation reduces rather than enhances teacher situational awareness.

Implication for the Foundation

Prioritize AI investments where the mechanism is human-AI collaboration — coaching support, real-time signals, feedback loops — over those claiming full automation of instructional or coaching functions. Require grantees to specify how AI outputs change human decisions, not just how they're generated.

Innovation

AI adoption in education is now widespread — but governance, data infrastructure, and policy conditions are not keeping pace

Signal strength
Strong

RPPL's RFP process found IRB approvals, data-sharing agreements, transcript permissions, and underdeveloped district AI guidance are materially slowing AI-enabled PL research. CRPE's Gap Analysis found barriers to AI's impact are not primarily technical but reflect misalignments in vision, incentives, and system design. EdReports, Transcend, and CRPE all separately identify that AI's presence doesn't mean responsible or effective implementation is happening.

Implication for the Foundation

Treat governance infrastructure — IRBs, data-sharing agreements, AI policy clarity — as a core diligence area for any AI-enabled investment, not administrative overhead. Support grantees in building this infrastructure proactively rather than encountering it as a blocker.

Innovation

AI efficiency gains in education are real but domain-specific — operational savings appear before instructional improvement, and conflating them overstates impact

Signal strength
Moderate

Multiple grantees are documenting AI efficiency: Springboard reduced logistics by 83%, FIND's iFIND cut video editing time 68%, and Waterford reports operational savings from distribution redesign. These are real gains. But the register notes a consistent pattern: AI is more immediately useful for operations and coaching logistics than for instructional product improvement or student learning. Leading Educators' national technology-use study found frequent tech-enabled practices didn't predict better school climate or student outcomes.

Implication for the Foundation

Ask AI grantees to separate efficiency claims by domain — operational, coaching/logistics, product development, and instructional — and require that efficiency gains in one domain not be used as evidence for impact in another.

Innovation

The tech backlash in schools is real and accelerating — but the right response is a purpose test, not a blanket rollback

Signal strength
Strong

At least 38 states have restricted cellphones; 17 are considering broader device limits; LAUSD imposed screen time caps on school-issued devices through elementary school. The backlash is bipartisan, culturally resonant, and gaining legislative velocity. But the evidence underlying it is messier than the political moment suggests — studies on cellphone restrictions show mixed effects on achievement and attendance, and the sweeping "screens harm children" narrative conflates social media harms with instructional edtech in ways the research doesn't support. The right frame, articulated in two OFF-authored pieces this quarter, is a purpose test: does this tool improve learning, help teachers, or advance a clear instructional goal? If not, it doesn't belong. Two grantee examples show the backlash is already reshaping product decisions: Zearn, responding to screen-time concerns affecting district adoption and renewal, is proactively making handwritten workbooks more visible alongside its digital platform — a signal that screen time is a means to an end, not the end itself. Leading Educators released VATT 2.0 and VATL, frameworks that help teachers and leaders assess when technology strengthens instruction and when it risks "fragmentation or distraction" — positioning LE as a guide through the backlash rather than a defender of the status quo. Separately, Instructure's 2026 EdTech Top 40 found districts maintain access to an average of 3,001 tools but interact with only four — tool sprawl is real, procurement and accountability are misaligned, and the market still rewards comprehensive solutions over better-evidenced focused ones. Notably, grantee renewal applications reveal a print/hybrid market signal that is distinct from political backlash: CommonLit reports that "the absence of consumable print workbooks is a consistent barrier in otherwise strong adoption cycles" — a procurement reality driven by districts with varied technology access, not screen-time politics. This is a meaningful distinction: the backlash and the market-fit gap are separate phenomena that call for different responses.

Implication for the Foundation

The backlash creates near-term adoption and renewal risk for edtech grantees — particularly those whose tools lack visible evidence or whose use cases can be characterized as "screen time" rather than "purposeful instruction." The Zearn and LE responses point to a portfolio-wide strategic move: grantees that proactively build and communicate their purpose case — through product design, frameworks, and evidence — are better positioned than those that stay silent. Ask every edtech grantee: how are you actively addressing the backlash environment, and what is your evidence of purposeful rather than passive use?

Implementation

Dosage is necessary but not sufficient — what moves outcomes is whether dosage is connected to a quality implementation system

Signal strength
Very strong

Across tutoring, PL, and edtech, the clearest Q2 learning is that dosage thresholds only matter when embedded in systems that include coherent materials, instructional leadership, coaching, and data routines. PLI's linear dosage-gain finding, Saga's 8-point Regents gains, and Magpie's jump from 24% to 57% at recommended dosage all reinforce the pattern — but only in contexts where supporting conditions were in place. Where conditions were absent (Baltimore vs. Houston in Magpie; low-support Saga Managed sites), dosage alone didn't hold. The Khan Academy India RCT adds the strongest external causal evidence: all schools had platform access, but only schools with a dedicated "lab-in-charge" — a named person with protected time, supervision responsibility, and troubleshooting ownership — converted that access into productive dosage and large math gains. Zearn's Year 1 Louisiana Lift lessons add the largest-scale live portfolio example: "alignment must span state → district → school → classroom, with regular touchpoints at each level" — the state partnership was foundational, front-loaded planning before schedules were finalized was the highest-leverage move, and leader coaching was only effective when structured around consistent routines and actionable data aligned across district and school levels simultaneously. A related nuance: rigid fidelity may be less useful than identifying the core causal features of a model and tracking whether those survive adaptation. PLI found traditional fidelity indices not useful across varied tutoring models; what mattered was whether the active ingredient — attended instructional time, relationship quality, or tutor responsiveness — was preserved.

Implication for the Foundation

When setting dosage expectations in grant design, pair them with explicit requirements for the enabling conditions — instructional leadership, coaching, data routines, and aligned materials — not just minimum minutes or sessions.

Implementation

Measurement activity is not the same as validated impact thresholds — grantees widely conflate participation in measurement with evidence of outcomes

Signal strength
Strong

Across portfolios, the register's most consistent caution is that grantees report measurement milestones — completed audits, shared toolkit pilots, dosage tracking systems — as if they validate impact. RPPL's shared measures work, FIND's Foundations completion rates, Waterford's growth reporting, and Quill's student engagement data are all meaningful leading indicators, but none establish causal links to outcomes. The Cross-Question Claim Map identifies this as a systemic pattern across the School Success Teacher portfolio.

Implication for the Foundation

In grant agreements and renewal conversations, explicitly distinguish measurement participation from measurement-to-impact evidence. Ask grantees to specify what would have to be true for their current measurement activity to constitute valid evidence of impact — and whether they're testing that.

Implementation

Scale strategies that reduce cost by reducing dosage, coaching intensity, or support create quality-outcome tradeoffs that are not yet resolved

Signal strength
Moderate

Multiple grantees are moving from high-touch direct delivery toward lower-cost models — Saga's three-model Progressive Services Approach, FIND's freemium entry, LENA's technology-enabled lighter-touch PreK model, and Public Impact's portal-supported blended services. All are plausible scale strategies. None has yet demonstrated that lower cost doesn't come with meaningful quality or outcome tradeoffs. In Saga's case, the New Mexico lower-cost model is being explicitly tested as a hypothesis, with the right design. In others, the transition is happening without pre-specified evidence gates.

Implication for the Foundation

Before supporting lower-cost scale transitions, require grantees to pre-specify minimum dosage and fidelity conditions for the new model, define what outcome decline is acceptable for cost reduction, and identify how they'll know if the tradeoff is unacceptable.

Implementation

The most common barriers to quality implementation are operational, human-capacity, and measurement failures — not instructional design flaws

Signal strength
Strong

Across 22 sources in the implementation synthesis, the recurring barriers to scale are not "the curriculum wasn't good enough" or "the model was wrong." They are: master scheduling that doesn't protect intervention time; tutor and teacher workforce instability; leadership turnover disrupting implementation ownership; IRB and data-sharing bottlenecks blocking research; legacy data systems that can't represent teams or shared rosters; and measurement frameworks that count access rather than receipt. PLI names scheduling, coordination, and inconsistent dosage definitions. ASU NEW adds a concrete architectural barrier — systems built around one teacher can't represent team-based exposure. ASSISTments, Jounce, PERTS, and Leading Educators all point to leadership ownership, feedback cycles, and role clarity as the binding constraints. The instructional design is usually not the problem. The operating conditions around it are. Two Q2 grantee responses add a specific dimension: physical presence is a scarce, non-substitutable implementation resource. EdLight found that as they scaled, in-person presence became "more, not less, important" — surfacing issues that analytics missed and building trust that sustains teacher adoption; they built regional contractor networks specifically to preserve it. Springboard's centralized virtual workshop pilot is the direct negative test: even with flexible scheduling and Spanish-language access, virtual delivery was not a viable substitute for in-person workshops among capacity-constrained partners.

Implication for the Foundation

Diligence should examine scheduling, staffing, leadership ownership, implementation support intensity, feedback cadence, data systems, and cost transparency before supporting scale — not just the instructional model. For any new grant with an ambitious reach goal, ask: what is the operational failure mode most likely to prevent the intended student experience from happening, and what is the mitigation plan?

Implementation

Feedback, monitoring, and quality assurance are plausible supports for dosage and quality — but only when paired with clear action routines

Signal strength
Moderate

PLI monitoring reports made dosage gaps and site variation visible — but visibility alone didn't close the gaps. OnYourMark and Saga built AI-enabled tutor feedback tools; ASU NEW built a Priority Practices Framework; ASSISTments built AI data summaries that increased assignment rates among the least-active teachers. In each case, the monitoring worked when it triggered a specific decision by a specific person within a specific timeframe. The synthesis is direct: ASU NEW's weakest Priority Practices domain was "Improve & Innovate Together" — collecting evidence is easier than building routines that act on it. Zearn's LiveView, EdLight's real-time dashboard, and ANet's Compass PLus all represent different grantees reaching the same design insight: the product has to close the loop from signal to action, not just surface the signal. COBC extends this principle to the contract itself: when OBC specifies dosage, data access, escalation triggers, and outcome definitions clearly enough, the procurement document becomes an action-forcing mechanism — and COBC's first edtech cohort produced dosage rates nearly 14x the industry baseline as a result. The open risk, surfaced in the Y2 board recommendation, is that only 31% of contracts achieved 60%+ of contracted outcomes, suggesting the action routine is not yet reliable beyond direct Center TA.

Implication for the Foundation

Fund monitoring and feedback infrastructure only when paired with a defined action routine: who reviews the data, what decisions are triggered, what the response timeline is, and how the resulting student experience will be verified. Treat dashboards, AI summaries, and observation tools as incomplete until the action chain is specified.

Implementation

AI is now a major cross-source implementation hypothesis — but the evidence base is still ahead on usage and workflow signals, and behind on verified student impact

Signal strength
Moderate

Teaching Lab, Saga, OnYourMark, Zearn, Khan Academy, Leading Educators, MCP, ASU NEW, and OBC all describe AI-enabled supports for feedback, coaching triage, data sensemaking, content refinement, and practice nudges. The Tutor CoPilot RCT is the strongest evidence: randomized at the tutor level, it found session-level mastery gains (+4 pp exit-ticket pass rate) and measurable tutor-practice shifts — but only 29% of treatment tutors actually used the tool, and there was no statistically significant end-of-year MAP achievement effect. That pattern — promising proximal signal, no confirmed durable impact — repeats across nearly every AI implementation investment in the portfolio. The synthesis adds two important cautions: most AI tools still operate within a one-teacher/one-classroom model and won't unlock team-based staffing unless redesigned for it; and AI efficiency claims tend to appear first in operations and coaching logistics, not in instructional outcomes. RPPL's 26–28 RFP process adds a research-quality dimension: most proposals submitted focused on testing specific AI tools rather than studying underlying PL design features — feedback, personalization, coaching structures, instructional coherence. This means the evidence infrastructure itself is misaligned with what the field actually needs to know. RPPL is actively redirecting proposals toward mechanism-level questions as a result.

Implication for the Foundation

Treat AI-enabled implementation as a learning agenda and research investment, not a solved problem. Grant language should require active-use definitions (not access), workflow baselines, practice-change measures, subgroup and equity checks, student-outcome links, safety guardrails, cost models, and independent evaluation when stakes are high. When a grantee claims AI saves time or improves efficiency, ask: in which domain — operational, coaching logistics, product development, or instructional — and what is the evidence?

Strategic partnerships

The hardest scale barrier is not demand — it's conversion, and conversion depends on affordability, buyer access, and implementation readiness, not program quality

Signal strength
Strong

Across tutoring, PreK PD, and edtech, grantees report strong field interest that fails to convert. FIND generated 600 new registrations via a free entry model; its paid pipeline remained thin. Saga's Saga Managed growth lagged despite strong demand signals. ANet found 80%+ of Compass interest came from existing partners. ROAR has a 2,000-school waitlist but paused expansion due to capacity constraints. In each case, the constraint was affordability, buyer-type mismatch, or partner readiness — not evidence or interest. The Collaborative Classroom case adds a related nuance: even financially self-sufficient grantees face a conversion ceiling — earned revenue alone cannot fund the product innovation speed needed to compete with well-capitalized for-profits, meaning philanthropy retains a role as R&D capital even for organizations that no longer need operating support.

Implication for the Foundation

Evaluate grantees' go-to-market strategies with the same rigor as their programs. The Foundation's relationships with district leaders and state officials may be a unique lever to unlock conversion that grantees can't access alone. For financially mature grantees, distinguish between operational funding (which they may not need) and R&D capital (which may be the remaining philanthropic value-add) when structuring renewal conversations.

Strategic partnerships

At least seven OFF grantee partnerships formed in a six-month window — the field is consolidating around HQIM coherence, and each partnership resolves a specific operational constraint rather than just aligning missions

Signal strength
Very strong

CC/AppleTree, Fishtank/Quill, Magpie/Great Minds, Zearn/MCP, Learning Commons/Magpie/ANet, Transcend/Gradient, and TalkingPoints/Illustrative Math all formed between January and March 2026. The mechanisms vary — distribution (CC/AppleTree), HQIM+AI embedding (Fishtank/Quill, Magpie/Great Minds), dosage unlock (Zearn/MCP), shared data infrastructure (Learning Commons), organizational absorption (Transcend/Gradient) — but the logic is consistent: each partnership closes a specific gap one party cannot fill alone. Zearn has dosage problems without a classroom redesign model; MCP has scaling problems without a platform already in millions of classrooms. Magpie has a strong K-8 literacy tool; Great Minds has curriculum distribution at scale. Notably, most of these formed without direct OFF brokering — the CC/AppleTree and CC/FluentSeeds cases (where OFF played a direct convening role) are the exceptions, not the norm. The field is moving on its own.

Implication for the Foundation

The partnership cluster signals a field-level recognition that no single organization can deliver coherent HQIM + digital tools + professional learning + data infrastructure alone. For the Foundation, two questions follow: (1) Which of these partnerships are evidence-generating bets vs. distribution arrangements — and are the right evidence gates in place? (2) Where the Foundation does broker partnerships (as in CC/AppleTree), are we investing in that convening function deliberately enough to replicate it?

Evidence adoption

Evidence is most likely to change practice when paired with structured engagement routines — dissemination alone rarely moves decisions

Signal strength
Strong

RPPL's structured audit routines with six PL organizations produced more actionable evidence adoption than downloads of the same Pocket Guide. EdReports' trust scores are high, but full-report engagement remains low — more users than ever, same share engaging with complete reviews. CRPE reached 1,000+ policymakers but can only document 27 concrete influence examples. Evidence adoption

Instructional coherence across Tier 1 materials, assessment, and professional learning is a prerequisite for most student outcome gains — but it's still the exception, not the norm

Strong

CCSSO's CICF early findings show teachers with more frequent, collaborative, student-centered PL report more positive HQIM implementation — but implementation quality is not correlated with teacher experience or self-reported use. EdLight, ANet, ASSISTments, and Zearn are all working on the same problem: tools that help teachers use data to act within — not just after — their core curriculum. Use instructional coherence — alignment across Tier 1 HQIM, assessment, and PL — as a core diligence lens. Ask grantees not just whether their product is HQIM-aligned, but whether the implementation conditions for coherent use are present in their target contexts.

Evidence adoption

The research-to-practice gap persists not because evidence is unavailable but because translation infrastructure — trusted messengers, decision-facing tools, local proof points — is missing or underfunded

Signal strength
Strong

SEDA found states want to know how their KRA policies fit nationally — but without a state liaison, that data never reaches decision-makers. Watershed's evidence session showed evidence must be embedded in the procurement process, not presented after decisions are made. ERS's cohort surfaced a state policy misunderstanding about the Blueprint 60/40 teacher time rule — sharing that back to MSDE changed guidance more than any published report. The Foundation's Q3 2026 Research Strategy formalizes this as three distinct mechanisms that all have to work simultaneously: translate (make evidence legible for non-researchers — Frameworks Institute as a communications partner for research grantees), deliver (route it to the actor who controls the relevant policy lever at the right moment — CCSSO for state HQIM adoption, NGA for chronic absenteeism in a gubernatorial election year, NCSL for PreK/math legislation), and enable action (practical tools that make it easy to use — ALI for state TA playbooks, OBC for procurement, DQC for data access). Most current evidence investments only address one layer.

Implication for the Foundation

Fund all three layers — translation, delivery, and action tools — with equal rigor, not just the production of evidence itself. Be intentional about which specific policy lever each evidence investment is meant to move, which actor controls that lever, and whether the delivery mechanism can actually reach them. A research product without a named decision-maker and a credible delivery path should not count as an evidence adoption strategy.

Evidence adoption

Evidence adoption is gated by who controls the relevant policy lever — routing evidence to the right actor at the right moment matters more than broad dissemination

Signal strength
Moderate

The Foundation's Q3 2026 Research Strategy (Strategy 3) makes explicit what grantee experience has been pointing to: field-level evidence adoption is not primarily a knowledge problem. It's a routing problem. The same evidence about HQIM adoption needs to reach state chiefs (CCSSO) for state curriculum policy, governors (NGA) for executive-branch absenteeism initiatives — where 36 gubernatorial elections in 2026 create a narrow leverage window — and state legislators (NCSL) for PreK and math statutes. Each actor controls a different lever, operates on a different timeline, and is persuaded by different messengers and formats. Disseminating to "policymakers" as a broad audience misses this. The evidence doesn't move until it reaches the specific person with authority over the specific decision, in a form they can use, at a moment they're open to acting. Stanford/NSSA makes the timing mechanism concrete: for mature approaches like tutoring, evidence aligns with decision-making when it is seeded ahead of leaders' budget and planning cycles — not timed to publication cycles. NSSA deliberately shares emerging insights before key budgeting moments so evidence is already in the room when allocations are being made. This reframes evidence adoption from a communications challenge into a targeting, timing, and seeding challenge.

Implication for the Foundation

Before funding an evidence adoption strategy, name the specific policy decision it is meant to move, identify who has authority over that decision, determine when that decision window opens, and match the delivery vehicle to that actor's information environment. A general report or op-ed rarely closes this loop alone. The Foundation's relationships with CCSSO, NGA, and state-level leaders are a direct asset here — convening power with the right decision-makers is translation infrastructure.
